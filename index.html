Influential 17th Century Mathematics: A Timeline

1609

Johannes Kepler

Johannes Kepler published the first two of his fundamental laws of planetary motion in Astronomia nova. His third was published in The Harmonies of the World in 1619. These were significant influences in the works of Sir Isaac Newton.

1614

John Napier

John Napier's discovery of logarithms to assist in multiplication of numbers with many digits is published in Mirifici logarithmorum canonis descriptio. Napier inspired the work of Henry Briggs, who developed the first table of common logarithms.

Read more
1619

Napier's kinematic model afforded the ability to visualize the curve of a particle moving through space, a significant concept in the development of calculus.

Read more
1620

Jost Bürgi

Jost Bürgi published the first table of geometric and arithmetic sequences.

Read more
1624

Henry Briggs

Henry Briggs continued Napier’s work following his death, publishing a table of common logarithms to the base of 10 (the Briggsian logarithm). He is credited with responsibility for Napier's logarithm tables being published and for driving their acceptance in the scientific community. He was also the voluntarily anonymous editor of books one through six of Euclid's Elements published in 1620.

Read more
1627

Bonaventura Cavalieri

Bonaventura Cavalieri wrote Geometria, a precursor to calculus (more specifically to integral calculus, basically finding the area under or between a curve, although this idea is also related to differential calculus).

Read more
1628

Adriaan Vlacq

Adriaan Vlacq produced a 10-place table that completed the missing values in Briggs' work. Together they produced trigonometric tables that later influenced spherical trigonometry.

Read more
1632

Cavalieri published Directorium Generale Uranometricum, which introduced logarithms to Italy. He also published Lo Specchio Ustorio, his work on problems of motion and optics that led some historians to claim he was the first inventor of the telescope.

Read more
1637

René Descartes

René Descartes published Le Monde with one appendix, La Géométrie, that would later become analytic geometry (which allowed equations to be solved using either algebra or geometry). His work also opened the door to future developments and discoveries that led to calculus.

Read more
1647

Blaise Pascal

Blaise Pascal, known for New Experiments Concerning Vacuums, did more than discover the existence of vacuums. His correspondence with Pierre de Fermat on the topic of the dice problem, studied by Girolamo Cardano, formed the foundation for probability theory.

Also notable is Pascal's wager from Pensées. Although philosophical, Pascal employs probability and mathematics to draw his conclusion to this argument: If God does not exist, one will lose nothing by believing in him, while if he does exist, one will lose everything by not believing...we are compelled to gamble...".

Read more
1666

Sir Isaac Newton

Sir Isaac Newton began his renowned work in mechanics and physics that was later to become his theory of universal gravitation. During this time, he formulated the beginnings of his three laws of motion. His inverse-square law was deduced from Kepler's third law of planetary motion and his own law of centrifugal force.

Read more
1670

Isaac Barrow

Isaac Barrow's Lectiones Geometricae is published by John Collins, which was pivotal in the later development of infinitesimal calculus.

Read more
1670

Pierre de Fermat

Pierre de Fermat's conjectured Fermat's Last Theorem is published by his son after his death, written in the margins of a translated version of Diophantus's Arithmetica. Although unproven until hundreds of years later, his theorem led to the discovery of commutative ring theory and the development of algebraic number theory. His work was also significant in the foundation of calculus.

Read more
1671

Newton wrote De Methodis Serierum et Fluxionum, the culmination of his work during the Great Plague of 1665-6. Newton is said to have pioneered the calculus of infinitesimals during this time, including finding the area under or between curves (integral) and rates of change and curve slopes (differential). This work was not published until 1736, the root of a fully unresolved intellectual war over who first invented calculus, Leibnitz or Newton. Some claimed Leibnitz plagiarized Newton's unpublished work. Others believed each developed their own ideas and methods, although they do at times translate to each other. During the Great Plague Newton also developed his theories on light, first published in 1672.

Read more
1679

Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz

While not published until 1703, Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz developed the binary system of arithmetic (without which we would have no computers or internet).

Read more
1684

Leibniz was also renowned for having published his differential calculus in the journal Nova Methodus pro Maximis et Minimis, itemque Tangentibus...in Acta Eruditorum, albeit without proofs. Two years later he published, in the same journal, a paper on integral calculus that included a first printing of the notation ∫.

Read more
1687

Newton published Philosophiae naturalis principia mathematica, thought by some historians to be the greatest scientific book known to man. This work, like Euclid's Elements, began with axioms, definitions, propositions, and theorems then offered geometric methods of reasoning (Dunlop, 2012, p. 193). It included Newton's law of gravitation, which explained the motions of planets and moons that Kepler had previously reduced to mathematical form (Kepler's third law of planetary motion).
